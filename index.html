Agentic Network Operations

The Agentic NOC Has Arrived.
Now the Real Engineering Begins.

Vinit Metange July 17, 2026 AI-Telecom Brief
TL;DR

Nokia, ZTE, and Ericsson put real agentic NOC deployments on the table at DTW Ignite 2026. The automation is working in bounded domains. What the industry still cannot ship is a production-grade trust boundary: the policy layer that defines exactly when an agent acts autonomously, when it escalates, and when human authorization is required. Closing that gap is what separates TMF Level 3 from Level 4.

The Lead

DTW26 Moved the Agentic NOC From Roadmap to Deployment

For two years, "agentic AI in network operations" was something operators said they were planning. June 2026 changed the framing.

At DTW Ignite in Copenhagen, Nokia announced a full Autonomous Networks Agent Library alongside a major upgrade to its Network Services Platform (NSP). The first production application is an AI-driven troubleshooting agent for IP networks: it qualifies incidents in under five minutes, lifts first-contact helpdesk resolution rates above 50%, and delivers what Nokia cites as 60-80% productivity gains compared to traditional NOC operations.

ZTE followed with a concrete demonstration of a path to TMF Level 4 autonomous networks, centering its case on agentic AI and cross-domain intelligence. Ericsson had already claimed the first independently validated Level-4 result under TM Forum's revised assessment-validation scheme, working alongside TDC NET in Denmark.

An NVIDIA AI-in-Telco survey published in early 2026 found that 89% of CSPs plan to increase their AI budgets over the next 12 months. Network automation overtook customer experience as the primary AI investment use case. 42% of CSPs have already deployed agentic AI for autonomous network resource management.

The agentic NOC is not a proof of concept. It is an integration project.

Deep Dive

The Three Layers Nobody Draws on the Same Diagram

The agentic NOC conversation habitually collapses two different engineering problems: collecting network intelligence and acting on it. Production deployments are discovering these are distinct problems with different failure modes, different standards surface, and different organizational ownership.

1
Perception Layer

This is NWDAF territory. The Network Data Analytics Function, standardized in 3GPP TS 23.288, has been enhanced through Release 18 and Release 19. Release 19 adds more than 23 analytics use cases, federated learning across decentralized NWDAF instances, analytics exchange in roaming scenarios, and improved accuracy reporting for ML models.

At the RAN layer, xApps on the Near-RT RIC handle sub-second analytics via the O-RAN E2 Interface. The Non-RT RIC passes policy targets to the Near-RT RIC via the O-RAN A1 Interface, making A1 the closest standards-defined approximation of a policy channel for RAN agents.

The critical gap: NWDAF produces probabilistic analytics outputs. An agentic system needs those outputs structured as actionable signals. Most deployments today hand-roll a translation layer between NWDAF and their agent framework. There is no standardized analytics-to-agent interface in the current specs.

2
Reasoning Layer

Production agentic NOC systems are not single-LLM deployments. Nokia's NSP framework explicitly supports "collaborative operations across agents." A supervisor agent receives an alert, delegates to specialized sub-agents (fault isolation, SLA impact assessment, remediation planning), consolidates their outputs, and presents a recommended action with a confidence score and supporting evidence.

TM Forum's Agentic NOC Catalyst Project (C26.0.924) defines this pattern: "a collaborative ecosystem of multi-domain intelligent agents spanning network, service, field force, security, analytics, and virtual operations." The key requirement is the multi-domain constraint. A single fault in an IP core can cascade into RAN congestion, transport latency, and enterprise SLA breach. The reasoning layer must span all three simultaneously.

3
Action Layer

This is where production deployments are most fragile, and where the industry is being least transparent about the hard problems.

Autonomous action in a live network means changes executed through O-RAN O1 (configuration management), O-RAN O2 (infrastructure management), PCF Policy Control Function APIs, or direct SMF session steering calls. These changes are fast, bidirectional, and sometimes operationally irreversible within the window of a service degradation event.

Trust Boundary / Policy Gate

TM Forum defines Level 4 autonomy as automated networks that "reason, act, and adapt independently, with minimal human involvement." Nokia's framing at DTW26 ("guided actions within defined policies and security boundaries") is the right architecture instinct. But a production trust boundary must answer three things precisely:

  1. Confidence threshold for autonomous action. Below what confidence score does the agent pause and escalate? This number must be domain-specific: a threshold for adjusting an energy-saving parameter differs from one for rerouting production traffic.
  2. Blast radius classification. Every proposed action should carry an estimated impact scope. High blast radius triggers mandatory Human-in-the-Loop, regardless of confidence score.
  3. Audit chain. Every autonomous decision needs a complete reasoning trace: what was observed, what alternatives were considered, why the chosen action was selected. This is both an operational necessity and, increasingly, a regulatory requirement.

TM Forum frames Level 4 as a shift from "systems assist humans" to "humans assist machines." That sentence is doing more work than it appears. It describes who bears responsibility for the outcome of a network change, and that is a governance and regulatory question as much as a technical one.

One Thing to Know

The Metric Nobody Is Publishing Yet

Nokia cited 60-80% productivity gains from its NSP agentic troubleshooting framework. That number is real, but it measures human labor saved in first-level triage, not network outcomes improved. The more important metric is mean time to restore (MTTR) in complex, multi-domain faults where an agent must coordinate across IP, RAN, and transport layers simultaneously. No vendor is publishing that figure yet. When they do, the trust boundary conversation will become measurable rather than philosophical.

Vinit's Take

The Architecture Work That Actually Matters in 2026

Vinit's Take

The agentic NOC debate in 2026 is dominated by capability announcements: faster incident qualification, higher resolution rates, bigger productivity multiples. Those are the right early-adoption proof points. But the architecture work that actually determines whether these deployments reach Level 4 and hold there is the trust boundary definition: blast radius thresholds, confidence floor policies, audit chain specifications. Nokia's "guided actions within defined policies" is the correct instinct. What the industry needs in the next 18 months is a reference implementation of what those policies look like at the specification level. That is the gap between a flagship demo and a production infrastructure. The operators that reach and hold Level 4 will be the ones that defined their blast radius policies before writing a single agent prompt.
